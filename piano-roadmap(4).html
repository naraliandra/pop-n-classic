Stage 1 — The absolute basics
Months 0–3 · 15–20 min/day · 12 weeks
Everything starts here. The goal is not to play beautifully — it's to build correct habits from day one. Bad posture and tension learned now will take months to undo later. Go slow, stay relaxed, and enjoy finding your way around the instrument.
Week 115 min/day
Meet the keyboard
Before touching music, learn the geography. The keyboard repeats in groups of 12 notes (7 white, 5 black). Black keys in groups of 2 and 3 are your landmarks — they let you find any white key without looking.
Name every white key A through G
Find all the C's on the piano (left of the 2-black-key group)
Find Middle C (centre of the keyboard)
Name black key groups — 2s and 3s
Sit at correct height: elbows level with keys
Curved fingers (like holding a ball), relaxed wrists

Daily drills

  • Close your eyes, land on any C — repeat 10 times
  • Tap every D on the keyboard from left to right, saying "D" aloud
  • Play any note slowly and listen until the sound fully fades
Week 215 min/day
Right hand + reading treble clef
The treble clef (top staff) is where your right hand lives. The five lines are EGBDF (Every Good Boy Does Fine) and the four spaces spell FACE. Place fingers 1–5 on C–D–E–F–G — this is your 5-finger position.
Memorize treble clef lines: E G B D F
Memorize treble clef spaces: F A C E
5-finger position RH: thumb on C, fingers on D E F G
Learn quarter note (1 beat) and half note (2 beats)
Play a 5-note melody by reading sheet music
Use Alfred's Adult All-in-One or Faber Adult Piano Adventures — they sequence note-reading perfectly so you're never guessing which note to learn next.

Daily drills

  • Point to a random treble clef note and name it in under 2 seconds
  • Play C–D–E–F–G and back, fingers 1–2–3–4–5–4–3–2–1, slowly and evenly
  • Count out loud while playing: "1, 2, 1–2" for quarters and halves
Week 315–20 min/day
Left hand enters + bass clef
The bass clef (bottom staff) is where your left hand lives. Lines are GBDFA (Good Boys Do Fine Always), spaces are ACEG (All Cows Eat Grass). Left hand 5-finger position mirrors the right: pinky on C below Middle C up through G. Playing hands together feels impossible at first — it's not. Go slower than feels necessary.
Memorize bass clef lines: G B D F A
Memorize bass clef spaces: A C E G
5-finger position LH: pinky on C, fingers on D E F G
Learn whole note (4 beats) and dotted half (3 beats)
Practice each hand separately for 5 min, then together for 5 min

Daily drills

  • LH alone: play C–D–E–F–G and back with fingers 5–4–3–2–1, then reverse
  • Hands together: both hands play the same note (C, D, E…) in parallel — go up, then down
  • Count out loud: "1-2-3-4" for whole notes, "1-2-3" for dotted halves
Week 420 min/day
First full piece
Put everything together. Choose "Ode to Joy" or "Mary Had a Little Lamb" — both stay within the 5-finger position and use notes you already know. The goal is to play from start to finish at a steady (slow) tempo without stopping. A wrong note you keep going through is better than stopping — stopping breaks rhythm, which is the real skill.
Review weeks 1–3 note names (both clefs)
Learn RH melody of "Ode to Joy" or "Mary Had a Little Lamb"
Add simple LH (block C or G notes on beat 1)
Play hands together, start to finish without stopping
Record yourself on your phone and listen back

Daily drills

  • Run the piece 3 times daily: once slow, once medium, once slow again
  • Identify the hardest bar — isolate it and play just that bar 5 times slowly
◆Milestone — play a complete piece, both hands, start to finish at a steady tempo
Week 520 min/day
Rhythm & the metronome
A metronome is your best teacher for the first year. It doesn't lie. Start at 60 BPM — slower than feels comfortable. Eighth notes divide a beat in half: two eighth notes = one quarter note. Clap and count rhythms before you play them — your body needs to feel the pulse before your hands express it.
Download a metronome app (Metronome Beats is free)
Clap quarter notes to the click at 60 BPM
Clap eighth notes: "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and"
Learn whole rest, half rest, quarter rest symbols
Play week 4 piece to a metronome at 60 BPM
Raise tempo only when 100% of notes are correct at the current speed. If you stumble, drop 5 BPM. Speed is earned, not forced.

Daily drills

  • Tap foot to metronome at 60 BPM while clapping a rhythm pattern: quarter, eighth, eighth, quarter, half
  • Play 5-finger scale with metronome: one note per click, then two notes per click (eighth notes)
Week 620 min/day
Dynamics & articulation
Music without dynamics is like speech without emotion. Piano (p) means soft — not pressing lightly, but playing with controlled weight. Forte (f) means loud — not banging, but pressing with arm weight behind the finger. Legato (smooth) connects notes so you barely hear the gap. Staccato (bounced) shortens each note to roughly half its value.
Play a phrase piano (p) — soft, controlled weight
Play same phrase forte (f) — arm weight, not banging
Legato: connect notes smoothly, one finger lifts as the next presses
Staccato: quick release, note lasts about half its written length
Learn crescendo (getting louder) and decrescendo (getting softer)
Play "Ode to Joy" with dynamics and slurs marked on your score

Daily drills

  • Play C–G scale legato up, staccato down — focus on even tone quality
  • Play any melody with a gradual crescendo from p to f over 4 bars, then decrescendo back
Week 725 min/day
Expand beyond 5 fingers
The thumb-under technique allows you to move across the keyboard without lifting your hand. As you play finger 3 (middle finger), the thumb tucks underneath and lands on the next note. This feels awkward for weeks — that's normal. Practise it slowly and without tension.
Learn ledger line notes just above and below the staff
Identify Middle C on both treble and bass clef
Thumb-under: RH plays C D E then thumb crosses under to play F
Play a piece or exercise that spans more than 5 notes
Identify ties vs slurs on sheet music

Daily drills

  • Slow thumb-cross: play 1-2-3, thumb under to 1, continue up — no wrist bump
  • Ledger line flashcards: draw 5 random ledger line notes, name them all
Week 825 min/day
Consolidation & review
This is the most important week of Month 2. Don't skip it. The goal is to polish what you have — not add more. A piece that feels 70% learned is not learned. This week, get two pieces to 95%+ and listen to the recording you made in week 4 to hear how far you've come.
Polish your best piece from weeks 4–7 until it feels automatic
Sight-read one new short piece you've never seen
Identify your weakest note-reading area (bass clef? ledger lines?)
Spend 10 min drilling that weakness specifically
Record yourself and compare to your week 4 recording

Daily drills

  • Play your piece from memory (eyes off the page) for just 8 bars
  • Name any note on a blank staff flashcard in under 3 seconds
◆Milestone — play a piece with clear dynamics, legato phrasing, and a steady metronome tempo
Week 925–30 min/day
Sharps, flats & key signatures
A sharp (♯) raises a note by one semitone (one piano key to the right). A flat (♭) lowers it by one semitone (one key to the left). Key signatures tell you which notes are always sharp or flat throughout the piece — you don't re-read the accidental each time. G major has one sharp (F♯). F major has one flat (B♭). C major has none.
Define sharp, flat, and natural on the keyboard
Play and name all 5 black keys as sharps and flats
Read a piece with accidentals (in-bar sharps or flats)
Identify C major, G major, F major key signatures
Learn the circle of fifths — just the first 4 positions

Daily drills

  • Play a short melody in C major, then add F♯ throughout — hear the shift in colour
  • Given a key signature on paper, name which notes are affected in under 5 seconds
Week 1030 min/day
C major scale — both hands
The major scale is the backbone of Western music. C major is all white keys, making it the clearest to learn. The fingering uses a thumb-under (cross) to cover 8 notes smoothly. RH: 1-2-3 then thumb under, 1-2-3-4-5. LH mirrors: 5-4-3-2-1 then finger 3 crosses over the thumb. The wrist should stay level — no bumping on the crossing.
C major scale RH fingering: 1 2 3 | 1 2 3 4 5
C major scale LH fingering: 5 4 3 2 1 | 3 2 1
RH alone: up and down one octave, smooth and even
LH alone: up and down one octave, smooth and even
Hands together: parallel motion, one octave
Understand: a half step is one key, a whole step is two keys
Even tone is the goal, not speed. Every note should be the same volume. Record yourself and listen for uneven notes — the thumb is usually the culprit.

Daily drills

  • Scale at 60 BPM, one note per click, focus on thumb crossing silently
  • Scale in groups of 4 notes, pause between groups to check hand position
Week 1130 min/day
Sight-reading as a daily habit
Sight-reading — playing a piece you've never seen before — is the skill that makes everything else faster. The key rule: never stop. Even if you miss a note, keep moving forward. Your eyes should be one bar ahead of where your hands currently are. Read ahead like reading a sentence, not a word at a time.
Eyes always 1 bar ahead — train this deliberately
Sight-read 3 new short pieces this week
Never stop for wrong notes — keep time
Before playing: scan the whole piece (key, time sig, highest/lowest note)
Set a 10-minute sight-reading slot in every practice session

Daily drills

  • Open method book to a new page — scan for 30 seconds, then play once at slow tempo
  • After playing: identify the 2 hardest bars and play them in isolation 5 times
Week 1230 min/day
Stage 1 finale
End Stage 1 by demonstrating everything you've built. Three polished pieces shows consistency. The scale shows technical control. Instant note naming shows reading fluency. The before-and-after recording is the real proof — and it will surprise you how much has changed in 12 weeks.
Play 2–3 pieces polished to performance level
C major scale HT at 72 BPM — clean, even, no bumping
Name any note on treble or bass clef within 2 seconds
Sight-read a piece one level above your comfort zone
Record a "Stage 1 complete" video and compare to week 4

What you've built

  • Correct posture and hand position — the foundation everything else rests on
  • Fluent note reading in both treble and bass clef
  • Rhythm: whole, half, quarter, eighth notes and their rests
  • Basic dynamics: piano, forte, legato, staccato
  • C major scale, both hands, with correct fingering
◆Stage 1 complete — you have the foundation. Stage 2 introduces harmony and chords.

Stage 2 — Chords & rhythm
Months 3–9 · 20–30 min/day · 24 weeks
Chords are the engine of music. Once you can build any major or minor triad, move between them smoothly, and play them over a bass line — you can accompany almost any song. This stage also expands your scale vocabulary and introduces rhythmic accompaniment patterns used in pop, classical, and jazz.
Weeks 13–1420–25 min/day
Major triads — building from the scale
A major triad is built by stacking: root + major 3rd (4 semitones) + minor 3rd (3 semitones). In C major: C (root) + E (major 3rd) + G (perfect 5th). The 1-3-5 shortcut works for every major key — count scale degrees, not semitones. Start with C, F, and G — the three chords that appear in every simple song.
Understand: triad = root + 3rd + 5th
Build C major, F major, G major triads in RH
Play each chord cleanly — all 3 notes together, no rolling
LH plays the root note while RH plays the full chord
Switch C–F–G–C smoothly without pausing
Build D, E, A, B major triads too

Daily drills

  • Chord cycling: C–F–G–C, 4 beats each, metronome at 60 BPM
  • Quiz yourself: call out a major key — build and play the triad within 5 seconds
Weeks 15–1625 min/day
Minor triads & the I–IV–V–I progression
A minor triad lowers the 3rd by one semitone: C minor = C + E♭ + G. The emotional contrast between major and minor is immediate and fundamental — train your ear to hear it. The I–IV–V–I progression underpins thousands of songs across every genre. In C major: C–F–G–C. Learning it now means you can fake-accompany most beginner songs immediately.
Build Am, Dm, Em, Bm minor triads
Play major and minor versions of the same chord — hear the difference
I–IV–V–I in C major: C–F–G–C, 4 beats each
RH plays melody, LH plays root notes of each chord
Accompany "Amazing Grace" or "Can Can" using only I–IV–V
When you hear a song and think "I know that chord pattern" — that's I–IV–V–I. It's the backbone of folk, pop, blues, and rock.
◆Milestone — accompany a simple song with I–IV–V–I chords, hands together
Weeks 17–2025–30 min/day
Chord inversions & voice leading
Inversions rearrange the notes of a chord so a note other than the root is on the bottom. C major root position: C-E-G. First inversion: E-G-C. Second inversion: G-C-E. Why does this matter? Voice leading — moving between chords with minimal hand movement. Instead of jumping your hand across the keyboard, inversions let you pivot smoothly. This is how professional pianists make chord changes sound seamless.
C major: root (C-E-G), 1st inversion (E-G-C), 2nd inversion (G-C-E)
F major and G major: all three inversions
I–IV–V–I using inversions for smooth voice leading
Hand should barely move between chords — aim for 1–2 note shifts
Add G major and D major triads in all inversions
Voice leading tip: when moving from C major to F major, keep E and G in the hand and just move C up to F. The hand barely moves. That's the goal.

Daily drills

  • Arpeggiate each chord (play notes individually bottom to top) in all inversions
  • I–IV–V–I with inversions at 72 BPM — smooth, no gaps between chords
Weeks 21–2430 min/day
Left hand patterns & Alberti bass
The Alberti bass is a LH accompaniment pattern used in nearly every classical sonata: instead of a block chord, you play root–5th–3rd–5th in a repeating cycle. It creates momentum without getting in the way of the melody. Broken chord arpeggios (playing chord notes in sequence) achieve a similar flowing effect. Both patterns are used in the Moonlight Sonata.
Alberti bass pattern LH: root–5th–3rd–5th, repeated
Broken chord arpeggio LH: root–3rd–5th–3rd, repeated
Block chord pattern: whole chord on beat 1, rest on 2–3–4
RH melody + LH Alberti bass simultaneously
Learn a pop song (e.g. "Let Her Go", "Clocks") using chord + melody

Daily drills

  • LH Alberti bass over C–Am–F–G, 8 beats each at 60 BPM — LH alone until effortless
  • Add RH five-finger melody while LH holds pattern — separate hands first, then together
Weeks 25–3030 min/day
All major scales + pentatonic
There are 12 major scales — one for each note. They all follow the same pattern of whole and half steps: W-W-H-W-W-W-H. Learning all of them builds finger muscle memory across all keys, unlocks any piece in any key, and trains your ear to recognise tonal centres. The pentatonic (5-note) scale is a simpler version used in pop, blues, and improvisation.
G major scale HT (1 sharp: F♯)
D major scale HT (2 sharps: F♯, C♯)
A major scale HT (3 sharps: F♯, C♯, G♯)
F major scale HT (1 flat: B♭)
B♭ major scale HT (2 flats: B♭, E♭)
C major pentatonic: C D E G A (skip F and B)
All scales at 80 BPM, two octaves if possible
Learn one new scale per week. Use the circle of fifths as your guide — each step clockwise adds one sharp, each step counter-clockwise adds one flat.

Daily drills

  • Scales in thirds: RH plays C-E simultaneously, then D-F, then E-G — good for even tone
  • Pentatonic improv: play only the 5 pentatonic notes over a C chord for 2 minutes — no wrong notes possible
◆Milestone — play any major triad in root position and all inversions, and name all 12 major scales

Stage 3 — Theory & ear training
Months 9–18 · 30–40 min/day
This is where playing by ear begins. Intervals are the atoms of music — every melody, every chord is made of intervals. Once your ear recognises them instantly, you start hearing music the way a chef tastes food: not as a whole experience but as components. 7th chords add colour and sophistication. Lead sheets let you play any song without full notation.
Weeks 37–4435 min/day
All 12 intervals — hearing the gaps
An interval is the distance between two notes. Each interval has a unique sound — once you know it, you can hear it anywhere. The trick to memorising them is song anchors: musical examples your ear already knows. Sing each interval before playing it. Your voice is the fastest path to your ear.
Minor 2nd (1 semitone) — Jaws theme
Major 2nd (2 semitones) — Happy Birthday
Minor 3rd (3 semitones) — Smoke on the Water
Major 3rd (4 semitones) — When the Saints Go Marching In
Perfect 4th (5 semitones) — Here Comes the Bride
Tritone (6 semitones) — The Simpsons theme
Perfect 5th (7 semitones) — Star Wars theme
Minor 6th (8 semitones) — The Entertainer
Major 6th (9 semitones) — My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean
Minor 7th (10 semitones) — Somewhere (West Side Story)
Major 7th (11 semitones) — Take On Me
Octave (12 semitones) — Somewhere Over the Rainbow

Daily drills

  • Use EarMaster or musictheory.net interval trainer — 10 min daily, ascending intervals first
  • Play an interval on the piano and sing it back before moving on
  • Sing the first two notes of each song anchor from memory
◆Milestone — identify any melodic interval by ear within 3 seconds
Weeks 45–5235 min/day
7th chords — adding the colour layer
A 7th chord adds a fourth note on top of a triad. The four types you need to know: Major 7th (bright, dreamy), Dominant 7th (tense, wants to resolve), Minor 7th (mellow, cool), Half-diminished (dark, jazzy). The ii–V–I progression uses three of these and is the most common chord movement in jazz — but it also appears in pop, R&B, and film music constantly.
Major 7th chord: Cmaj7 = C-E-G-B (root + maj3 + perf5 + maj7)
Dominant 7th: G7 = G-B-D-F (root + maj3 + perf5 + min7)
Minor 7th: Dm7 = D-F-A-C (root + min3 + perf5 + min7)
Half-diminished: Bm7b5 = B-D-F-A (root + min3 + dim5 + min7)
ii–V–I in C: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7
Hear: major 7 = dreamy · dom 7 = tense · min 7 = mellow
Play ii–V–I in 3 different keys (C, F, G)
The dominant 7th (G7) has a strong pull toward the I chord (C). That tension-release is the engine behind most chord movements in Western music. Once you hear it, you hear it everywhere.
◆Milestone — hear a chord and distinguish major, minor, or dominant 7th by ear
Weeks 53–6040 min/day
Reading lead sheets — play any song
A lead sheet shows only the melody (in notation) and chord symbols above it (like "Cmaj7", "G7", "Am7"). You create the rest — the bass line, the voicing, the rhythm pattern. This is how professional musicians at jam sessions can play any song with any band. Your job: RH plays melody, LH interprets the chord symbols creatively.
Understand a lead sheet: melody + chord symbols only
Decode chord symbols: Cmaj7, Dm7, G7, Am, Fsus2
RH plays written melody, LH plays root note of each chord
Upgrade LH: play full block chord on beat 1 of each bar
Upgrade further: LH plays broken arpeggio pattern
Find a lead sheet for a song you love and play through it

Daily drills

  • Download 3 lead sheets from musicnotes.com or hooktheory.com — try each one cold
  • For each chord symbol: build it in under 3 seconds without looking it up
◆Milestone — play a full song from a lead sheet with no prior preparation, on the first try

Stage 4 — Chord vocabulary
Months 18–30 · 30–45 min/day
This is where your playing starts to sound sophisticated. Extended chords (9ths, 11ths, sus), modern voicings, and the ability to transcribe songs by ear — these skills unlock any genre. Transcription is the secret weapon: figuring out chords and melodies by ear, even imperfectly, trains your ear faster than any exercise.
Months 19–2235–40 min/day
Extended chords: 9ths, sus, add9
Extended chords add notes beyond the 7th. A 9th chord = 7th chord + 9th (same as the 2nd, one octave higher). Sus chords suspend the 3rd and replace it with the 2nd or 4th — creating tension that "resolves" when the 3rd returns. These are the chords behind the modern pop sound: Coldplay, Radiohead, John Legend all rely on them heavily.
Dominant 9th: G9 = G-B-D-F-A
Major 9th: Cmaj9 = C-E-G-B-D
Sus2: Csus2 = C-D-G (3rd replaced by 2nd)
Sus4: Csus4 = C-F-G (3rd replaced by 4th)
Add9: Cadd9 = C-E-G-D (triad + 9th, no 7th)
Play a pop progression using Csus2–G–Am–Fsus2
◆Milestone — identify a sus, add9, or major 9 chord by ear in a song you're listening to
Months 23–2640 min/day
Chord voicings — the pro sound
Voicing is the art of arranging the notes of a chord across the keyboard for maximum musical effect. A Cmaj7 can be voiced as C-B-E (shell + 7th + 3rd) in the LH while the RH plays the melody. Or G-B-E across both hands. Professional pianists spend years exploring voicings — they're what make two people playing the same chord sound completely different.
Shell voicings: LH plays root + 7th, RH plays 3rd + extension
Drop-2 voicings: lower the 2nd voice from top by one octave
Quartal voicings: stack 4ths instead of 3rds (D-G-C-F)
Voice a ii–V–I in C with smooth voice leading between chords
Play the same chord 4 different ways and hear the difference
Great voicings create space. You don't need to play every note of a chord — leaving notes out often sounds richer than playing them all.
Months 27–3045 min/day
Transcription — training your ear by hand
Transcription means figuring out a piece of music by listening — no sheet music. Start with just the bass line (lowest note per chord). Then identify chord quality (major/minor). Then figure out the full voicing. Even 70% correct transcription trains your ear enormously. Use Transcribe! software or YouTube videos slowed down with the speed function.
Transcribe the bass line of a simple pop song
Identify chord quality (major/minor/dom 7) for each chord
Write out the chord chart (just symbols, no notation)
Play your transcription back against the recording — adjust where wrong
Transcribe one song per week for this entire phase

Song suggestions to transcribe

  • "Let Her Go" by Passenger — simple I–V–vi–IV progression throughout
  • "The Scientist" by Coldplay — 4-chord loop, slow tempo, clear bass
  • "Hallelujah" by Leonard Cohen — I, IV, V, vi with a few passing chords
◆Milestone — transcribe the chord chart of a full song by ear, verified against a real chart

Stage 5 — Playing by ear
Months 30–48 · 45 min/day
You've been building toward this. Playing by ear is not about special talent — it's about having enough musical patterns stored in your ear and hands that when you hear something, you recognise the parts faster than you need to think. This stage trains the real-time connection between what you hear and what you play.
Months 31–3645 min/day
Advanced ear training — melodic dictation
Melodic dictation means listening to a melody and writing (or playing) it back exactly. Harmonic dictation means identifying each chord by ear. These are high-level skills that feel frustratingly hard at first — but they're the difference between needing sheet music and not needing it. Daily practice of 10–15 minutes compounds fast.
Melodic dictation: hear a 4-bar melody and play it back
Harmonic dictation: name each chord in a 4-bar progression
Identify chord quality: major, minor, dom 7, maj7, min7, dim
Identify chord inversions by ear (which note is on the bottom)
Sing back a melody after one listen — no piano
Use EarMaster or Teoria.com for 15 min daily
The goal: when you hear a chord change, you feel it before you think it. Like recognising a friend's face — instant, automatic, no calculation.
◆Milestone — hear a chord played once and name it (quality + root) within 2 seconds
Months 37–4245 min/day
Improvisation foundations
Improvisation is the act of composing in real time. The secret beginners don't know: you only need 5 notes to start. The pentatonic scale (C-D-E-G-A) works over nearly any C major chord progression — no wrong notes. Then add the blues note (G♭) for soul. Target chord tones (1-3-5-7 of each chord) on the strong beats (1 and 3) and use passing notes in between.
Improvise freely using only C pentatonic over I–IV–V backing track
Add the blues note (G♭) — feel the extra tension
Target chord tones on beats 1 and 3 of each bar
Call-and-response phrasing: play 2 bars, "rest" 2 bars, answer yourself
Improvise for 5 continuous minutes without stopping
Transpose your improv to G major and F major
The goal of improvisation at this stage is not to sound impressive — it's to connect what you hear in your head to what your hands play. Start slow and melodic. Speed and complexity come later.
Months 43–4845 min/day
Playing along & faking it
"Faking" means creating a convincing-sounding arrangement of any song on the spot — without sheet music, without preparation. You hear the key, you feel the time signature, you recognise the progression, and your hands play. This is the output of all the pattern-recognition you've built. Practice it daily: put on a song you've never played and try to join in within 8 bars.
Play along to a recording on first listen — don't stop
Identify the key of any song within 15 seconds of hearing it
Transpose a song you know well into 3 different keys
Fake a full arrangement (melody + harmony + bass) on the spot
Play a song you've never seen sheet music for
Jam with another musician (guitarist, singer) without preparation

Daily challenge

  • Pick a random song from a playlist you've never played — play along within the first chorus
  • After playing: name the key, write out the chord progression from memory
◆Milestone — hear a song once and play it back with melody and chord accompaniment

Stage 6 — Instant play
The destination · 4+ years · ongoing
Stage 6 is not a finish line — it's the beginning of a lifetime of musical conversation. The patterns you've built are now automatic. What used to require thought now flows. You sit at a piano and things come out. The work now is deepening style, expanding repertoire, and playing with others.
What it looks like
You hear it — you play it
Someone hums a melody. A song comes on the radio. A friend asks you to play something. You sit down — and it comes out. Not perfectly, but recognisably, musically, fluently. That's the destination. Here's what got you there:
Recognise common progressions instantly (I–IV–V, ii–V–I, I–V–vi–IV)
All 12 major and natural minor scales — no hesitation
Fluent in 2+ styles (pop, jazz, classical, gospel…)
Arrange songs in real time — melody, harmony, bass, rhythm simultaneously
Improvise freely over any chord progression

What keeps you growing at Stage 6

  • Learn a new style: stride piano, bossa nova, gospel — each expands your vocabulary
  • Transcribe solos from pianists you admire: Oscar Peterson, Bill Evans, Chick Corea
  • Play with musicians better than you — the fastest growth environment
  • Teach someone else — explaining forces you to articulate what you know intuitively
Stage 6 is not about perfection — it's about fluency. Like speaking a language: you don't think about grammar, you think about what you want to say. The music is the conversation.

Making it stick

Principles that matter

Daily beats weekly

15 focused minutes every day produces faster results than 2 hours on weekends. Your brain consolidates motor patterns during sleep — the gap between sessions is part of the learning.

Never look at your hands

From week one, resist looking down while you play. Navigate by feel. This pays enormous dividends later when your eyes need to read music.

Slow is smooth, smooth is fast

Every piece should be learned at a tempo where zero mistakes happen. Only then raise the metronome. Fast and stumbling embeds the wrong patterns.

Record yourself weekly

The gap between what you think you sound like and what you actually sound like is wide at first. Recordings close that gap faster than anything else.
